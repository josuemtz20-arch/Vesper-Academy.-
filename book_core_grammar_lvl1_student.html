Lesson 1: The Parts of Speech

Context
The Suffix Game:
Can you change the word 'Compete' into a noun, an adjective, and an adverb?
Study
Vocabulary:
Noun (n): A word used to identify any of a class of people, places, or thingsPronoun (n): A substitution for a nounAdjective (adj): A word that describes a nounVerb (v): Action performed by the subjectAdverb (adv): Describes a verb, adjective, or another adverbPreposition (prep): Connects nouns to another part of the sentenceConjunction (conj): Connects two similar elements and ideasInterjection (int): An expression of emotion
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
To build complex sentences, you must understand exactly how words function. A word can completely change its function depending on its suffix.

1. NOMINALIZATION (Verbs to Nouns)
Structure: Verb + Suffix (e.g., -tion, -ment, -ance) = Noun.
Examples:
- The scientists decided... → The scientists made a decision.
- The analysis of the data was important.

2. ADVANCED ADVERBS
Concept: Use adverbs to link ideas logically, not just to describe verbs.
Examples:
- Consequently, the experiment failed.
- Furthermore, the results were incredible.
Exercises
Task 1: Change Verb to Noun
1. They decided to investigate... → They made a ________ to launch an ________.
2. The government regulated the industry. → The ________ of the industry.

Task 2: Advanced Adverbs
3. The budget was cut; (therefore / furthermore), the project was delayed.
4. He was tired; (nevertheless / moreover), he completed the manuscript.
Your Turn
Take a simple paragraph ('He did the test. It was good.') and elevate it using nominalizations and formal adverbs.

Lesson 2: Simple Past vs Present Perfect

Context
Interview: Ask a partner: 'When did you graduate?' (Past Simple) vs. 'How many books have you read this year?' (Present Perfect).
Study
Vocabulary:
ever (adv): Sometime in the pastago (adv): Meaning 'in the past', counted from the presentlast (adv): Similar to 'previous'since (prep): To express the moment something startedfor (prep): To express the duration something has hadyet (adv): Something hasn't been done BY that momentjust (adv): Happened a very short time agonever (adv): Opposite of always
Grammar Note:
1. SIMPLE PAST
Concept: Used for historical facts or actions that happened in the past and finished. They do not continue.
Structure: Subject + Verb in Simple Past + (Complement)
Examples:
- Christopher Columbus discovered America in 1492.
- I broke up with my ex-boyfriend three years ago.

2. PRESENT PERFECT
Concept: Used for actions that connect the past with the present, or an action at an unspecified time.
Structure: Subject + have/has + Verb (Past Participle) + (Complement)
Examples:
- I have worked for this company for 10 years.
- My mom has told me that.

Note: Never use a specific finished time word (ago, yesterday) with the Present Perfect.
Exercises
Task 1: Verb Conjugation
1. In 1998, the company (go) ________ bankrupt.
2. Over the last decade, technology (alter) ________ the way we communicate.
3. Dr. Allen (not publish) ________ any new research since 2015.

Task 2: Error Correction
4. Throughout history, humanity has faced many plagues in the 14th century.
5. I have finished the report yesterday afternoon.
Your Turn
Write a short summary of AI history. Use Past Simple for the 1950s, and Present Perfect for recent events.

Lesson 3: Narrative Tenses (Past Continuous & Perfect)

Context
Timeline Puzzle: Connect two events: 'Server crashed' / 'Updating software' using narrative tenses.
Study
Vocabulary:
background (n): Context or surrounding details specially from the pastongoing (adj): Still happening now at the same timewhile (conj): At the same timewhen (conj): At that momentbefore (conj): Earlier than something happensafter (conj): Later than something happensby the time (exp): Before something happensalongside (adj): Next to / together
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Case studies require complex timelines to show depth and sequence instead of just basic Simple Past.

1. PAST CONTINUOUS (The Background)
Concept: Sets the scene. Describes ongoing conditions interrupted by other events.
Structure: Subject + was/were + Verb-ing
Examples:
- While the market was collapsing, competitors were innovating.
- I was going to school when it started to rain.

2. PAST PERFECT (The Past of the Past)
Concept: Shows sequence. Marks that one action happened BEFORE another past action.
Structure: Subject + had + Verb (Past Participle)
Examples:
- The company had already lost millions before the CEO resigned.
Exercises
Task 1: Gap Fill
1. While the engineers (test) ________ the engine, a fire (break out) ________.
2. As they (enter) ________, the alarm (sound) ________.

Task 2: Sentence Combining
3. The researchers analyzed the samples. Before that, the freezer broke. → When the researchers analyzed the samples, the freezer ________.
Your Turn
Investigate a failure. Report what was happening leading up to the crisis, and what had already gone wrong.

Lesson 4: Future Forms for Predictions

Context
Discuss the difference between 'It will rain someday' and 'It is going to rain now (look at clouds!)'
Study
Vocabulary:
forecast (n): A calculation or estimate of future eventspredict (v): Say or estimate what will happen in the futureanticipate (v): Regard as probable; expectschedule (n): A plan for carrying out a processlikelihood (n): The state or fact of something's being likelyimminent (adj): About to happententative (adj): Not certain or fixeddefinitive (adj): Final, not able to be changed
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
In English, we don't just use "will" for everything. The degree of certainty changes the grammar.

1. WILL
Concept: Hypotheses, opinions, or distant futures.
Structure: Subject + will + Base Verb
Examples: AI will likely alter job markets in the next century.

2. BE GOING TO
Concept: Evidence-based forecasts. It's happening soon based on visible data.
Structure: Subject + am/is/are + going to + Base Verb
Examples: Look at the graph; temperatures are going to rise.

3. PRESENT CONTINUOUS
Concept: Fixed, scheduled arrangements with a set date/time.
Structure: Subject + am/is/are + Verb-ing
Examples: We are presenting our paper next Tuesday.
Exercises
Task 1: Choose the Form
1. We (fly) ________ to Tokyo next Monday. (We have tickets).
2. Look at those dark clouds; it (rain) ________.
3. I personally believe that humanity (colonize) ________ Mars by 2050.

Task 2: Rewrite for Certainty
4. We have a meeting tomorrow. → We ________ tomorrow.
Your Turn
Present a business plan. Use Present Continuous for next month, 'going to' for 1-year data, and 'will' for a 5-year vision.

Lesson 6: Stative vs Dynamic Verbs

Context
Mind Reader: Discuss why states of mind are permanent and cannot be 'in progress'. (E.g. can you 'do' a belief?)
Study
Vocabulary:
stative (adj): Expressing a state or condition rather than an activity or eventdynamic (adj): Characterized by constant change, activity, or progressperceive (v): Become aware or conscious of somethingconsist of (v): Be composed or made up ofresemble (v): Have qualities or features, especially those of appearance, in common withpossess (v): Have as belonging to one; owntangible (adj): Perceptible by touch
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Continuous tenses (-ing) are typically reserved for actions (Dynamic verbs). Stative verbs describe states, thoughts, ownership, and senses. They usually CANNOT take an -ing form.

1. PURE STATIVE VERBS
Concept: Possession, knowledge, abstract thought.
Examples: belong, own, possess, know, believe, understand.
- We understand the risks. (Not: We are understanding)

2. MIXED VERBS
Concept: Some verbs act differently depending on the context.
Examples:
- Have: I have a car (State = possession). I am having a meeting (Action).
- Think: I think it's true (State). I am thinking about it (Action).
Exercises
Task 1: Choose the correct form
1. The committee (consists / is consisting) of five core members.
2. Currently, the CEO (thinks / is thinking) about restructuring.
3. I absolutely (understand / am understanding) your concern.

Task 2: Meaning Shifts
4. I weigh 70kg. vs. I am weighing the chemicals.
5. I see what you mean. vs. We are seeing changes in the data.
Your Turn
Describe a patient's current mental state. Use 'believe', 'know', 'resemble' correctly alongside what they 'are doing'.

Lesson 7: Modals of Ability & Permission

Context
The Bouncer: What is the difference between being physically 'able' to enter a club vs. being 'authorized' to enter?
Study
Vocabulary:
authorize (v): Give official permission for or approval toempower (v): Give (someone) the authority or power to do somethingprohibit (v): Formally forbid (something) by law, rule, or other authoritycapacity (n): The maximum amount that something can containcompetence (n): The ability to do something successfully or efficientlyclearance (n): Official authorization for something to proceed or take place
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
'Can' and 'Could' are too basic for formal writing. We use formal equivalents to specify exactly the type of ability or permission.

1. ABILITY (Physical/Intellectual Capacity)
Structure: be able to / have the capacity to / be capable of + V-ing.
Examples: The AI is capable of analyzing gigabytes per second.

2. PERMISSION (Authority/Legal Rights)
Structure: be authorized to / be permitted to / may.
Examples:
- Employees are prohibited from accessing the server.
- The researcher was granted clearance to view it.

3. PAST ACHIEVEMENT
Structure: managed to.
Examples: We managed to isolate the virus. (Instead of 'could isolate').
Exercises
Task 1: Upgrade the Modal
1. The students can use the lab after hours. → The students are ________.
2. The system can process heavy loads. → The system is ________.
3. Nobody can enter without an ID. → Personnel are ________.
4. She could solve the equation yesterday. → She managed ________.
Your Turn
Write a formal memo to the IT department outlining what Level 1 staff 'are authorized to do' versus what they 'lack the capacity to alter'.

Lesson 8: Modals of Obligation & Advice

Context
What do mountain climbers 'have to' do to survive, and what 'ought' they to do for comfort?
Study
Vocabulary:
oblige (v): Make someone legally or morally bound to an action or course of actionadvice (n): A suggestion or recommendationmandatory (adj): Required; must be doneoptional (adj): Not required; you can chooseadvisable (adj): A good idea; recommendedprudent (adj): Careful and wiseprohibit (v): To completely forbid something
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Distinguishing between what is legally required, optionally permitted, and highly recommended.

1. STRONG OBLIGATION
Concept: Required by rule or internal force.
Structure: Must / Have to / Be required to.
Examples: Staff are required to wear badges.

2. LACK OF OBLIGATION & PROHIBITION
Concept: Optional vs strictly forbidden.
Structure: Don't have to (optional) vs Must not (forbidden).
Examples: You don't have to attend (optional). You must not enter (forbidden).

3. ADVICE / RECOMMENDATION
Concept: Highly formal suggestions.
Structure: It is highly advisable to / strongly recommended that / ought to.
Examples: It is prudent to invest early.
Exercises
Task 1: Insert Obligation / Prohibition
1. This data is classified. You (must not / don't have to) ________ share it.
2. Filing reports in triplicate is an old policy; you ________ do it anymore.
3. All new employees ________ complete the safety orientation.

Task 2: Upgrading Advice
4. You should back up your data. → It is highly advisable ________.
5. I think you ought to read this book. → It is strongly recommended ________.
Your Turn
Draft a remote work policy. Include 2 mandatory rules, 1 prohibition, 1 optional rule, and 1 strong recommendation.

Lesson 9: First & Second Conditionals

Context
The Dreamer vs. Realist: Realist: 'If I pass, I will graduate.' Dreamer: 'If I won the lottery, I would buy the school.'
Study
Vocabulary:
provided that (conj): Used to say what must happen or be done to make it possible for something else to happenunless (conj): Except if; if notscenario (n): A postulated sequence or development of eventsfeasibility (n): The state or degree of being easily or conveniently donevariables (n): An element, feature, or factor that is liable to vary or changeoptimal (adj): Best or most favorablecompromise (v): Make a deal between different parties where each gives up part of their demand
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Conditionals form the basis of all hypotheses and contingency plans.

1. FIRST CONDITIONAL (Real/Possible Future)
Concept: Likely outcomes based on a real condition.
Structure: If + Present Simple, Will + Base Verb
Examples: If the market stabilizes, we will launch.
- Use Provided that or As long as instead of 'If' for formal demands.

2. SECOND CONDITIONAL (Unreal/Hypothetical Concept)
Concept: Imagining a different reality.
Structure: If + Past Simple, Would + Base Verb
Examples: If we had infinite funding, we would build on Mars.
- Always use 'were' instead of 'was' (If I were the CEO...).
Exercises
Task 1: Verb Conjugation
1. If they (lower) ________ the price tomorrow, demand (increase) ________.
2. If I (be) ________ in charge, I (restructure) ________ everything.
3. We (hire) ________ more staff if we (have) ________ the space, but we don't.
4. Provided that the test results (be) ________ positive, we (proceed) ________.

Task 2: Rewrite using 'Unless' or 'Provided that'
5. If you don't wear gear, you cannot enter. → Unless...
6. If you sign the NDA, you can read the file. → Provided that...
Your Turn
Roleplay a negotiation. Use 1st conditionals for actual terms ('Provided that you give us 20%...') and 2nd conditionals for hypotheticals.

Lesson 11: The Passive Voice

Context
Ego Eraser: Erase 'I' and 'We'. 'I analyzed the data' → 'The data was analyzed.'
Study
Vocabulary:
undergo (v): Experience or be subjected to (something, typically something unpleasant, painful, or arduous)implement (v): Put (a decision, plan, agreement, etc.) into effectmanipulate (v): Handle or control (a tool, mechanism, etc.), typically in a skillful mannerobjective (adj): (of a person or their judgment) not influenced by personal feelings or opinions in considering and representing factsrigorous (adj): Extremely thorough, exhaustive, or accuratevia (prep): Traveling through (a place) en route to a destination
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
In scientific writing, who did the action is usually irrelevant. What matters is the process and the result. The Passive Voice creates an objective tone.

STRUCTURE
Object + BE (in tense) + Verb (Past Participle)

EXAMPLES
- Present: The samples are tested daily.
- Past: The experiment was conducted in 2020.
- Present Perfect: The results have been verified.
- Modals: Funding must be secured.

Note: Only use 'by [Agent]' if the doer is surprising. ('The theory was proven by Einstein').
Exercises
Task 1: Active to Passive Transformation
1. The technicians test the water quality every hour. → The water quality ________.
2. They had fired the manager before the scandal broke. → The manager ________.
3. We have gathered all the necessary materials. → All the necessary materials ________.
4. You must submit the application by Friday. → The application ________.

Task 2: Find the Error
5. The thesis was wrote by a brilliant student.
6. The components are being manufacture in China.
Your Turn
Write a 'Methodology' section for testing coffee on cats using exclusively passive voice.

Lesson 12: Reported Speech

Context
Telephone: Partner A: 'I ate pizza.' Partner B: 'He claimed he had eaten pizza.'
Study
Vocabulary:
assert (v): State a fact or belief confidently and forcefullyclaim (v): State or assert that something is the case, typically without providing evidenceclarify (v): Make (a statement or situation) less confused and more comprehensibleemphasize (v): Give special importance or prominence to (something) in speaking or writingverbatim (adv): In exactly the same words as were used originallyparaphrased (adj): Express the meaning of (the writer or speaker or something written or spoken) using different words
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
When discussing other people's research, we report what they said indirectly. This requires 'backshifting' (moving the tense one step back into the past).

STRUCTURE (Backshifting)
- Present → Past: "I am right." → He stated that he was right.
- Past → Past Perfect: "We found a flaw." → They claimed they had found a flaw.
- Will → Would: "It will work." → She asserted that it would work.

TIMESTAMP SHIFTS
- 'Tomorrow' → 'The next day' | 'Yesterday' → 'The previous day'

Exception: Do not backshift universal truths. ("Einstein said the speed of light is constant").
Exercises
Task 1: Backshifting Tenses
1. "We are analyzing the newly discovered fossils." → Dr. Grant noted that they ________.
2. "The server crashed completely yesterday." → The IT team reported that the server ________ ________.
3. "I will present my findings tomorrow." → She stated that she ________ ________.

Task 2: Universal Truths (Backshift or not?)
4. Galileo said, "The earth revolves around the sun."
5. The teacher said, "Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius."
Your Turn
Take a transcript of a fiery debate. Write a summarized report stating exactly what they accused each other of, properly backshifted.

Lesson 13: Relative Clauses

Context
Compare: 'My brother who lives in NY is rich' vs 'My brother, who lives in NY, is rich.' How many brothers do I have in each?
Study
Vocabulary:
specify (v): Identify clearly and definitelyrestrict (v): Put a limit on; keep under controlelaborate (v): Develop or present (a theory, policy, or system) in detailsuperfluous (adj): Unnecessary, especially through being more than enoughvital (adj): Absolutely necessary or important; essentialpertaining to (prep): Belonging or relating to
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Relative clauses use pronouns (who, which, that, where) to combine sentences. The punctuation completely changes the legal/academic meaning.

1. DEFINING CLAUSES (No Commas)
Concept: Essential information. Identifies exactly WHO or WHAT.
Examples: "The students who cheated were expelled." (Only the cheaters were expelled. You can use 'that').

2. NON-DEFINING CLAUSES (With Commas)
Concept: Parenthetical, extra detail. The subject is already clear.
Examples: "The students, who cheated, were expelled." (ALL students were expelled; oh, and by the way, they all cheated.)
- NEVER use 'that' after a comma in these clauses.
Exercises
Task 1: Pronoun Selection
1. The laboratory ________ we conduct our research is secure.
2. The professor ________ theory won the prize is retiring.
3. The new software, ________ cost millions, is obsolete.

Task 2: Comma Insertion (Defining vs Non)
4. Oxygen which is essential for life makes up 21% of the atmosphere.
5. The laptops that are stored in the server room are strictly for staff.

Task 3: Find the Error
6. The Eiffel Tower, that is located in Paris, was built in 1889.
Your Turn
Read a poorly punctuated contract clause. Explain how the lack of commas legally changes which exact assets are being seized by the bank.

Lesson 14: Used to / Be used to / Get used to

Context
Discuss moving to a foreign country. 'I used to eat bread, but now I'm getting used to rice. I am used to it now.'
Study
Vocabulary:
accustom (v): Make (someone or something) accept something as normal or usualhabituate (v): Make or become accustomed or used to somethingacclimate (v): Become accustomed to a new climate or to new conditionsconventional (adj): Based on or in accordance with what is generally done or believednovel (adj): New or unusual in an interesting waystatus quo (n): The existing state of affairs
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
These three structures look identical but serve completely different grammatical functions regarding habituation.

1. USED TO + Base Verb
Concept: Past Habit. True in the past, but NOT anymore.
Examples: We used to code in Python, but now we use Rust.

2. BE USED TO + Noun/V-ing
Concept: Current condition. It is normal for me.
Examples: The doctors are used to working 10-hour shifts.

3. GET USED TO + Noun/V-ing
Concept: The psychological PROCESS of adapting.
Examples: The software is confusing, but you will get used to it soon.
Exercises
Task 1: Selection
1. Before the promotion, I ________ have much more free time.
2. The weather is brutal, but I will eventually ________ it.
3. The astronaut is entirely ________ experiencing zero gravity.

Task 2: Form Conjugation
4. I am used to (manage / managing) large teams.
5. The startup used to (operate / operating) out of a garage.

Task 3: Transformation
6. It was normal for him to smoke, but he stopped. → He ________ smoke.
Your Turn
Address your team after a harsh corporate buyout. Discuss what they 'used to do', what they 'must get used to', and what they 'are already used to'.

Lesson 16: Gerunds vs Infinitives

Context
Discuss 'Stopping TO smoke' vs 'Stopping smoking'. Meaning matters.
Study
Vocabulary:
hesitate (v): Pause before saying or doing something, especially through uncertaintyneglect (v): Fail to care for properlytolerate (v): Allow the existence, occurrence, or practice of (something that one does not necessarily like)anticipate (v): Regard as probable; expect or predictstrive (v): Make great efforts to achieve or obtain somethingreluctance (n): Unwillingness or disinclination to do something
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
When two verbs collide, the first verb dictates the form of the second. This is memory-based, with some patterns.

1. VERBS + GERUND (V-ing)
Concept: Finished actions, enjoyment, or avoidance.
Examples: suggest, avoid, risk, postpone, tolerate. ('We avoided testing it').

2. VERBS + INFINITIVE (To + Base Verb)
Concept: Future plans, goals, or agreements.
Examples: agree, refuse, plan, intend. ('We agreed to fund it').

3. VERBS THAT CHANGE MEANING
Examples: Remember, Stop.
- Remember + V-ing: Recall a past memory. ('I remember locking the door').
- Remember + To: Remembering a task. ('Remember to lock the door').
Exercises
Task 1: Pattern Recognition
1. The committee decided (funding / to fund) the department.
2. We strongly suggest (reviewing / to review) the protocols.
3. They postponed (launching / to launch) the satellite.

Lesson 17: Contrast Connectors

Context

Study
Vocabulary:
although (conj): In spite of the fact that; even thoughdespite (prep): Without being affected by; in spite ofnevertheless (adv): In spite of that; notwithstanding; all the samecontradiction (n): A combination of statements, ideas, or features of a situation that are opposed to one anotherparadox (n): A seemingly absurd or self-contradictory statement or propositioncaveat (n): A warning or proviso of specific stipulations, conditions, or limitations
Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Expressing contrasting ideas is core to critical thinking. The grammar depends on what follows the connector.

1. FOllOWED BY A FULL CLAUSE (Subject + Verb)
Structure: Although / Even though.
Examples: Although the prototype failed, the theory is sound.

2. FOLLOWED BY A NOUN/GERUND (No Verb)
Structure: Despite / In spite of.
Examples: Despite the failure, the theory is sound.

3. INDEPENDENT SENTENCE CONNECTORS
Structure: However / Nevertheless. (Requires punctuation).
Examples: The prototype failed. However, the theory is sound.

Grammar Note:
CONCEPT
Locating exactly *when* and *where* a variable occurred is critical in research or incident reports.
