Lesson 21: The Discursive Essay

Key Concepts

1. The 4-Paragraph Structure (The Gold Standard)

  • Intro (40-50 words):
    A) Hook (Paraphrase Topic): "The debate regarding the subsidization of tertiary education is contentious."
    B) Thesis Statement: "This essay will argue that while free tuition fosters equality, the economic burden is unsustainable."
    C) Outline: "Both perspectives will be analyzed."
  • Body 1 (The View You Disagree With or 'On the one hand'):
    Topic Sentence -> Explanation -> Example -> Concession ("However, this ignores...").
  • Body 2 (The View You Support or 'On the other hand'):
    Topic Sentence -> Explanation -> Example -> Reinforcement.
  • Conclusion (40 words):
    Summary -> Final Verdict. "In conclusion, despite the social benefits, the financial implications make universal free education impractical."
Examiner Psychology Examiners look for 'Coherence & Cohesion'. Using 'signposting' words (Conversely, Furthermore, Consequently) is essential, but overusing them looks mechanical. Use them naturally at the start of clauses.

2. Advanced Linkers

  • To Add: Moreover, Furthermore, In addition to this, It is also worth noting that...
  • To Contrast: Conversely, On the contrary, Be that as it may, Notwithstanding...
  • To Conclude: In light of the above, Taking all factors into account...

Lesson 22: The Formal Report

Key Concepts

1. Structural Requirements

A report MUST have a title and subheadings. Without them, you lose marks for 'Communicative Achievement'.

Title: Report on the Decline of Office Productivity
Introduction: "The aim of this report is to analyze..."
Findings: "It was observed that..." / "Data suggests that..."
Recommendations: "It is strongly advised that..."

2. The Power of the Passive

  • Instead of "We interviewed 50 people", use: "Interviews were conducted with a cohort of 50."
  • Instead of "Everyone complained", use: "Widespread dissatisfaction was expressed regarding..."
Advanced Grammar Use the Subjunctive in recommendations:
"It is recommended that the manager be informed." (Not 'is informed').
"It is vital that the system remain operational." (Not 'remains').

3. Useful Phrases

  • Intro: The purpose of this report is to outline... / This report aims to evaluate...
  • Findings: According to recent statistics... / The majority of respondents indicated... / A significant discrepancy was noted between...
  • Ending: Recommendations include... / The following measures should be implemented...

Lesson 23: The Proposal

Key Concepts

1. The Persuasive Angle

Unlike a report, a proposal needs to sell an idea. You must clearly state the benefits of your plan.

Structure:
1. Introduction (Current Situation + Problem).
2. Proposed Method (What we will do).
3. Expected Benefits (Why it's good).
4. Conclusion (Call to action).

2. Key Vocabulary for Proposals

  • Justification: "The rationale for this initiative is..."
  • Benefits: "This would undoubtedly result in..." / "The long-term advantages include..."
  • Feasibility: "It is a cost-effective solution..." / "The logistical requirements are minimal..."
Hypotheticals Use 'Would' and 'Could' extensively. "Implementing this plan would increase revenue." Avoid 'Will' unless certain.

Lesson 24: Review Writing

Key Concepts

1. The Hook & Voice

Start with a rhetorical question or a bold statement. "Have you ever wanted to walk out of a cinema after 5 minutes?"

2. Compound Adjectives (The Secret Weapon)

  • Positive: Breathtakingly beautiful, World-renowned, Thought-provoking, Highly-acclaimed, Mouth-watering.
  • Negative: Over-hyped, Cringe-worthy, Ill-conceived, Disappointingly shallow, Eye-wateringly expensive.

3. Structure:

  • Intro: Literal details (What, Where, When).
  • Summary: Brief plot/description (No spoilers!).
  • Analysis: The Good vs The Bad.
  • Verdict: Who is this for? "I would wholeheartedly recommend this to..."

Lesson 25: Speaking Pt 2: Long Turn

Key Concepts

1. The Strategy (Compare, Answer, Speculate)

Do NOT just describe the photos ("There is a woman..."). Compare them immediately.

Formula: "While the first picture depicts a stressful office environment, the second contrastingly shows a relaxed remote working setup."

2. Speculation (When you don't know)

Examiners love modal verbs of deduction.

  • "They must be feeling exhausted..."
  • "It's highly likely that they are..."
  • "I hazard a guess that..."

3. Buying Time (Fillers)

"That's an intriguing question...", "Let me see...", "It's difficult to say for certain, but..." (Use these instead of 'Uhhh').

Lesson 26: Speaking Pt 3: Collaboration

Key Concepts

1. Collaborative Phrases

  • Soliciting Opinion: "What's your take on this?", "How does that strike you?", "Would you go along with that?"
  • Agreeing: "You've hit the nail on the head.", "I couldn't agree more.", "That's a valid point."
  • Disagreeing: "I see where you're coming from, but...", "I beg to differ...", "That's debatable."

2. Moving the Conversation Forward

"Shall we move on to the next point?", "Let's consider the issue of...", "We seem to be on the same page regarding X, but what about Y?"

Examiner Tip Do not dominate the conversation. You get marks for inviting your partner to speak.

Lesson 27: Reading: Skim & Scan

Key Concepts

1. Skimming (Gist)

Read the Title, the First Sentence (Topic Sentence) of every paragraph, and the Final Sentence (Concluding thought). Skip the middle. This gives you the skeleton of the argument.

2. Scanning (Specifics)

Let your eyes float over the text looking for shapes (Capital letters, Numbers, Italics). Do not sub-vocalize (read aloud in your head).

3. Dealing with Unknown Words

Context clues: Is it a noun? Is it positive or negative? If it's not in the question, ignore it.

Lesson 28: Listening: Distractors

Key Concepts

1. The Correction Strategy

Speakers often state an answer, then negate it immediately.
"I was thinking of taking the train... but looking at the prices, the bus is the only option." (Answer: Bus, not Train).

2. Synonyms in Questions

The question paper will NEVER use the same words as the audio. They use synonyms.
Audio: "The layout was confusing."
Question: "The speaker found the design bewilderling."

3. Quantifiers/Frequency

Watch out for absolute words like 'All', 'None', 'Always'. The audio usually expresses nuance ('Most', 'Rarely').

Lesson 29: Use of English: Word Formation

Key Concepts

1. Common Transformations

  • Negatives: Legible -> Illegible, Relevant -> Irrelevant, Mature -> Immature.
  • Nouns -> Adjectives: History -> Historic (Famous) / Historical (Old).
  • Verbs -> Nouns: Maintain -> Maintenance, Pursue -> Pursuit, Abstain -> Abstinence.

2. Exceptions & irregularities

Destroy -> Destruction (Not Destroyment).
Strong -> Strength.
Long -> Length.
High -> Height (No 'e' at end of high, but 'e' in height).

Lesson 30: Use of English: Transformations

Key Concepts

Common Exam Patterns:

  • Comparative: "He is the best player." -> "No other player is AS good AS him."
  • Passive: "People say he is rich." -> "He IS SAID TO BE rich."
  • Conditionals: "I didn't study, so I failed." -> "IF I HAD studied, I WOULD HAVE passed."
  • Preference: "I prefer tea to coffee." -> "I would RATHER have tea THAN coffee."
  • Habit: "He used to smoke." -> "He was IN THE HABIT OF smoking."
