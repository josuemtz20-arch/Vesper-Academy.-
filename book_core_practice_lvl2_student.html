Course Overview:

Level 2 focuses on Intermediate Communication & Survival (A2-B1). This module utilizes the Deep Expansion Strategy to provide a rigorous, massive 1-hour classroom experience.

Classroom Flow:

  • Odd Lessons (Auditory): Speaking and Listening focus. Extended, split audio inputs with rigorous 3-stage warmups and deep negotiation roleplays.
  • Even Lessons (Textual): Reading and Writing focus. Long-form, multi-paragraph reading passages mapping true B1 comprehension, followed by formal 3-paragraph essay drafting tasks.

Lesson 1: Small Talk

Warm Up
1. The Elevator Pitch: You have 30 seconds to introduce yourself to a stranger in an elevator. What do you say? Go!

2. Taboo Topics: Brainstorm 5 topics you should NEVER discuss during small talk in your country (e.g., salary, politics). Discuss why.

3. The Icebreaker: Ask your partner: 'What is the most awkward conversation you have ever had with a stranger?'
Vocab Drill
Core Arsenal (12 phrases):
1. Break the ice (Initiate conversation)
2. Catch up (Share recent news)
3. Bump into (Meet by chance)
4. Bring up (Introduce a topic)
5. Run out of things to say (Have no more topics)
6. Silence is deafening (Awkward silence)
7. Small talk (Polite, casual conversation)
8. Talk shop (Discuss work socially)
9. Speak your mind (Say what you think)
10. Get to the point (Be direct)
11. Put your foot in it (Say something embarrassing)
12. Mingle (Socialize at a party)

Drill task: Choose 3 phrases. The teacher will count down from 3, and you must say a completely spontaneous sentence using them.
Main Listening Input
Press play and listen twice. The words are not printed here on purpose — train your ears! Then answer the questions below.
Comprehension Check
Level 1 (Literal):
- Where do David and Sarah run into each other?
- Did Sarah go to Italy?

Level 2 (Inference):
- Why did David say 'Let's not talk shop'? What did he want to change the topic to?
- How does Sarah try to 'break the ice' again after the awkward moment?

Level 3 (Applied Opinion):
- Do you think David was rude to ask about the trip? How would you react in his position?
Guided Practice
Exercise 1: Phrase Replacement: Replace the underlined words with our vocabulary: 'I accidentally said something embarrassing.' -> 'I accidentally put my foot in it.'

Exercise 2: Question Formation: Write 3 open-ended questions designed specifically to 'break the ice' at a networking event.

Exercise 3: Intonation Focus: Read David's apology aloud. Focus on lowering your pitch and sounding genuinely empathetic: 'I am so sorry, Sarah. I completely put my foot in it.'
Free Output Simulation
The Nightmare Networking Event (15 mins)

Setting: A boring corporate party. You MUST keep the conversation going for 3 full minutes without stopping.

Role A: You are very talkative but keep 'putting your foot in it' by accidentally asking highly personal or inappropriate questions. Try to 'talk shop' constantly.
Role B: You are very private and hate talking about work. You must skillfully deflect Role A's questions and change the subject back to safe 'small talk' like hobbies or the weather.

Win Condition: If there is a silence longer than 5 seconds, you both lose.

Main Reading Input
PART 1: The Email
Dear Customer Service Department,

I am writing to express my profound dissatisfaction with my recent stay at the Grand Plaza Hotel (Booking Reference: 8945A) from October 12th to October 14th. The level of service I received was frankly appalling and completely unacceptable for a facility claiming to be a luxury establishment.

Upon arrival, despite having guaranteed my reservation with a credit card, I was informed that my premium suite was unavailable. I was relegated to a standard room next to the elevator shaft, which vibrated loudly every ten minutes.

PART 2: The Demand
Furthermore, the air conditioning in the room was defective. When I brought this to the attention of the front desk, the receptionist was dismissive and downright incompetent, stating that 'maintenance had already gone home.' No alternative room or fan was offered.

Given the misleading advertising on your website regarding 'world-class service' and 'tranquil rooms', I am formally requesting a 50% refund as compensation for this miserable experience. I expect you to acknowledge receipt of this email promptly and resolve this matter within five business days before I escalate my complaint to the Consumer Rights Board.

Sincerely,
Jonathan Davies
Comprehension Check
Level 1 (Literal):
- What was Jonathan's booking reference?
- What was wrong with the room's location?

Level 2 (Inference):
- Why did Jonathan use the word 'relegated'? What does it imply about his feelings?
- Why did the author put 'world-class service' in quotation marks?

Level 3 (Applied Opinion):
- Is asking for a 50% refund reasonable in this situation? Should he have asked for 100%?
Guided Practice
Exercise 1: Tone Shifting: Convert these angry thoughts into formal complaint email phrases:
- 'Your staff didn't care.' -> 'The staff was highly dismissive.'
- 'Fix this now.' -> 'I expect you to resolve this promptly.'

Exercise 2: Gap Fill: 'I am writing to express my profound ______. The product was severely ______ and the customer service was ______. I demand immediate ______.'

Exercise 3: Threat Mitigation: How does Jonathan threaten the hotel without using bad words? Identify the specific phrase used at the end of the email.

PART 1: The Acceleration of Innovation
In the span of a single century, humanity has leaped from the first powered flight to the dawn of artificial intelligence. Today's cutting-edge technologies are not just improving our lives; they are fundamentally rewiring human behavior. Automation and state-of-the-art robotics have streamlined industries, drastically reducing the need for human manual labor.

However, this rapid technological breakthrough comes at a steep sociological cost. Device dependency has become arguably the greatest behavioral crisis of the modern era. PART 2: The Dark Side of Convenience

Lastly, our 'user-friendly' digital world requires us to surrender our personal data freely. Massive privacy breaches are no longer anomalous events; they are routine. We must question whether the convenience of a smart-home and an algorithmically curated life is truly worth the absolute sacrifice of our anonymity.

PART 1: The Death of Nuance
The landscape of modern journalism has undergone a radical and arguably destructive transformation. In the pursuit of ad revenue and engagement metrics, many media outlets have abandoned objective reporting in favor of sensationalism and clickbait. Complex geopolitical issues are reduced to 280-character arguments, killing any sense of nuance.

Furthermore, social media algorithms deliberately trap users inside 'echo chambers'. These codes are designed to track what you read, and then constantly feed you articles that confirm your existing biases. You are rarely challenged by opposing viewpoints, which leads to intense polarization and political extremism in society.

PART 2: The Responsibility Problem
The burden of truth has systematically shifted from the editor to the consumer. In the past, journalistic integrity demanded that facts were verifiable and cross-checked before printing. Today, amateur blogs and anonymous social media accounts spread sophisticated propaganda in seconds.

To navigate this digital minefield, consumers must develop severe media literacy. We must learn to distinguish between opinion pieces and hard news, identify manipulative emotional language, and cross-reference stories across global sources before we hit the 'share' button.

Main Reading Input
PART 1: The Trap of Lifestyle Creep
One of the most insidious financial traps modern professionals face is known as 'lifestyle creep'. This phenomenon occurs when an individual's standard of living improves identically as their income rises. They secure a high-paying promotion, but rather than investing the surplus, they immediately upgrade to a luxury car, secure a larger mortgage, and splurge on designer goods. As a result, despite earning a six-figure salary, they remain perpetually living paycheck to paycheck, accumulating liabilities instead of assets.

PART 2: The Magic of Compound Interest
True financial independence demands radical discipline. Financial literacy experts argue that wealth is not built through salary alone, but by aggressively acquiring assets early in life. The mathematical secret is compound interest. By diversifying investments across mutual funds and stocks during your twenties, even modest amounts of money begin to generate their own income streams over decades.

However, this requires living a highly frugal lifestyle while your peers are buying status symbols. It forces a harsh psychological choice: do you want to look rich now, or actually be wealthy in the future?

Prompt: Write a letter to future students who are just starting Level 2.

Task: Write a sophisticated 3-paragraph essay.
- Paragraph 1: Congratulate them on starting, but warn them about the 'Plateau' and the difficulties of intermediate grammar.
- Paragraph 2: Share specific advice on how to 'overcome' listening or speaking anxiety using the 'perseverance' you developed.
- Paragraph 3: Paint a picture of how 'empowering' it feels to finally be 'proficient' at the end of the course.
